You just landed a new job. Congratulations — seriously. Now comes the part nobody warns you about: writing your **LinkedIn new job announcement** without making everyone in your network quietly cringe. It sounds easy. It is not. Somewhere between "I'm excited to share" and the final hashtag, most people lose their way and end up writing something that reads like an acceptance speech at the Oscars — for a mid-level marketing role.

But here's the thing: a well-written LinkedIn job announcement actually matters. It sets the tone for your new chapter, introduces you to future colleagues, and keeps your professional network in the loop. The trick is doing it without sounding like you just won the Nobel Prize for switching companies.

Let's fix that.

## Why Your LinkedIn Job Announcement Matters

Your LinkedIn new job announcement is often the first thing your new colleagues, clients, and industry contacts will see about you in this new role. It's a digital handshake — and first impressions stick.

**It shapes how new colleagues perceive you.** Before your first day, people at your new company will look you up. Your announcement is a preview of your personality. A thoughtful, grounded post signals someone who's easy to work with. A 12-paragraph saga about your "journey" signals... something else.

**It keeps your network warm.** People you've worked with want to know where you've landed. A simple, genuine update keeps those connections alive without forcing anyone to read your autobiography.

**There's a real difference between genuine and performative.** We've all seen the posts. The ones that start with "I am BEYOND humbled" and end with 47 tagged names. You can feel the algorithm-chasing from a mile away. Genuine excitement reads differently than manufactured emotion, and your network can tell the difference instantly.

The goal isn't to go viral. The goal is to share your news like a normal human being.

## The Anatomy of a Perfect Job Announcement

Before we get to templates, let's break down what actually belongs in a LinkedIn new job announcement — and what doesn't.

### What to Include

**Your new role and company.** This sounds obvious, but you'd be surprised how many people bury the actual news under six paragraphs of backstory. Lead with the information people came for.

**A genuine reason you're excited.** Not "I'm excited about the synergistic opportunities for growth." Something real. Maybe it's the product, the team, the mission, or honestly just that you get to work on problems you care about.

**Gratitude that feels real.** Thank the people who actually helped. Your former manager who wrote you a recommendation. The friend who referred you. One or two specific acknowledgments land harder than a list of 30 tagged names.

**What you're looking forward to.** Give people a reason to root for you. What are you hoping to build, learn, or contribute? Keep it to a sentence or two.

### What to Leave Out

**The "humbled and grateful" opener.** You're not humbled. You applied, interviewed, negotiated, and accepted an offer. That's the opposite of humbling. You're excited. Say that instead.

**Tagging your entire network.** Tagging 30 people in your announcement to boost engagement isn't gratitude — it's a distribution strategy. Tag people only if you're directly thanking them for something specific.

**The multi-paragraph origin story.** Nobody needs to know about your childhood dream of working in B2B SaaS. If your career path is genuinely unusual and adds context, a sentence or two is fine. Three paragraphs is a memoir.

**Performative vulnerability about the "journey."** If you were laid off and bounced back, that's legitimately tough and worth acknowledging — briefly. But manufacturing a dramatic arc out of a normal job search ("After months of soul-searching and self-discovery...") isn't vulnerability. It's content creation.

## 5 LinkedIn Job Announcement Templates

Here are five templates you can actually use. Each one fits a different situation, so pick the one that matches your vibe and adapt it.

### Template 1: The Simple & Direct

**Structure:**

> Big news — [role] at [company].
>
> [One sentence about why you're excited.]
>
> [One sentence about what you're looking forward to.]
>
> [Optional: brief thanks to someone specific.]

**Example:**

> Big news — I'm joining Stripe as a Senior Product Manager.
>
> I've been a fan of how they approach developer tools for years, and I'm genuinely thrilled to be part of the team building the next generation of payment infrastructure.
>
> Looking forward to diving in next month. If you're at Stripe, say hi — I'm the new person who will be asking too many questions in Slack.

**When to use it:** This is your go-to for most situations. It works if you're moving to a well-known company, staying in your field, or just want to keep things clean. It respects your audience's time and still gets the job done. Most LinkedIn job posts could benefit from this level of brevity.

### Template 2: The Grateful (But Not Too Grateful)

**Structure:**

> Excited to share that I'm starting as [role] at [company].
>
> [One sentence about the role or company.]
>
> I want to especially thank [1-2 specific people] — [brief, specific reason].
>
> [Forward-looking sentence.]

**Example:**

> Excited to share that I'm starting as a Design Lead at Figma next month.
>
> I'll be working on the FigJam team, which has been one of my favorite collaboration tools since it launched.
>
> I want to especially thank my former manager Sarah Chen — her mentorship over the past three years shaped how I think about design leadership, and her recommendation meant the world.
>
> Can't wait to start building.

**When to use it:** Use this when someone genuinely helped you get here and you want to acknowledge them publicly. The key is specificity — say *why* you're thanking them, not just *that* you're thanking them. This template to announce a new job on LinkedIn works especially well when you have a strong mentor relationship worth highlighting.

### Template 3: The Storyteller

**Structure:**

> [Brief career context — 1-2 sentences max.]
>
> That's why I'm thrilled to be joining [company] as [role].
>
> [What specifically draws you to this role.]
>
> [Forward-looking sentence.]

**Example:**

> I've spent the last eight years building data platforms at startups — first at a 10-person team where I was the entire data department, then scaling analytics at a Series C company to support 200+ internal users.
>
> That's why I'm thrilled to be joining Databricks as a Staff Data Engineer. The chance to work on the tools I've been using (and occasionally cursing at) for nearly a decade is something I couldn't pass up.
>
> Day one is April 7th. If you're working on interesting data problems, I'd love to connect.

**When to use it:** This works when your background adds meaningful context to the move. The rule of thumb: if the career context explains *why* this role makes sense, include it. If it's just a chronological summary of your LinkedIn profile, skip it. People can read your experience section themselves.

### Template 4: The Team Player

**Structure:**

> I'm joining [company] as [role] — and here's what got me excited about this team.
>
> [2-3 sentences about the team, mission, or what they're building.]
>
> [What you hope to contribute.]

**Example:**

> I'm joining Notion as a Senior Engineer — and here's what got me excited about this team.
>
> They're rethinking how teams organize knowledge, and the engineering challenges involved in making something that flexible also feel simple are genuinely fascinating. I talked to six people during the interview process, and every single one of them made me want to work here more.
>
> I'm hoping to bring my experience with real-time collaboration systems and help push the product forward. First day is next week — let's go.

**When to use it:** This is great when the company or team is the main draw. It subtly demonstrates that you did your homework and made a thoughtful choice. It also makes your new colleagues feel good, which isn't a bad way to start. This style of LinkedIn new job announcement works well for mission-driven companies.

### Template 5: The Career Changer

**Structure:**

> After [X] years in [previous field], I'm making a move that might surprise some of you.
>
> I'm joining [company] as [role].
>
> [Why this transition makes sense — be specific, not vague.]
>
> [What you're bringing from your previous experience.]

**Example:**

> After six years as a management consultant, I'm making a move that might surprise some of you.
>
> I'm joining Loom as a Product Manager.
>
> The short version: I spent the last two years consulting for tech companies and realized I wanted to be on the building side, not the advising side. I completed a product management program, did a few side projects, and eventually got the chance to interview at Loom — a product I've been using daily since 2021.
>
> I'm bringing my experience in stakeholder management, data analysis, and surviving 14-hour consulting days. The last one might be the most transferable skill.
>
> Nervous and excited in equal measure. Here we go.

**When to use it:** When your new role LinkedIn announcement involves an industry or function change, people will have questions. This template answers the obvious ones upfront. Keep the explanation tight — you don't need to justify yourself, just give enough context that the transition makes sense. A touch of humor helps if it comes naturally.

## Real Examples: From Cringe to Classy

Let's look at what separates a LinkedIn job post that makes people smile from one that makes them scroll faster. Here are three side-by-side comparisons.

### Example 1: The Over-the-Top vs. The Authentic

**The cringe version:**

> I am BEYOND humbled, grateful, blessed, and honored to announce that after an incredible journey of self-discovery and growth, I will be joining Amazon as a Program Manager!! This opportunity is truly a dream come true and I cannot begin to express the depth of my gratitude to every single person who has been part of my journey. From my kindergarten teacher Mrs. Robinson who first told me I was a leader, to my college roommate who believed in me when I didn't believe in myself...
>
> *(continues for 400 more words)*

**The good version:**

> Really excited to share that I'm joining Amazon as a Program Manager next month. The team is working on some fascinating logistics challenges, and I can't wait to dig in. Grateful to everyone who supported me through the process — you know who you are. Let's go!

**What changed:** The good version conveys the same information and genuine excitement in 90% fewer words. The emotion is real because it's not performing. Notice how "you know who you are" is actually more meaningful than tagging 30 people — it implies real relationships, not LinkedIn engagement farming.

### Example 2: The Humble Brag vs. The Honest Share

**The cringe version:**

> I never thought a kid from a small town in Ohio who grew up with nothing, was the first in their family to go to college, and was rejected from 47 jobs before getting their first break would end up as a Senior Engineer at Google. This just goes to show that ANYTHING is possible if you never give up on your dreams. To every person out there who's struggling — keep going. Your time will come. #NeverGiveUp #DreamBig #GoogleLife

**The good version:**

> Joining Google as a Senior Engineer next month. It's been a long road — career switches, plenty of rejections, and a lot of late nights studying. But I'm here now, and I'm ready to build. Special thanks to my wife for tolerating my whiteboard interview prep taking over the living room for three months.

**What changed:** Both versions acknowledge a challenging path. But the cringe version turns a job announcement into a motivational poster. The good version is honest without being preachy. The specific detail about the whiteboard in the living room makes it feel human. If you want to announce a new job on LinkedIn in a way that resonates, specificity beats inspiration every time.

### Example 3: The Tag-Everyone vs. The Focused Thanks

**The cringe version:**

> I couldn't have done this without my amazing support system!! Thank you @John @Sarah @Mike @Lisa @David @Jennifer @Tom @Amy @Chris @Rachel @Kevin @Michelle @Brian @Laura @Steve @Nicole @Mark @Emily @Dan @Ashley @Rob @Megan @Eric @Jessica @Jake @Amanda @Tyler @Brittany @Alex @Sam for being part of this incredible journey!!!

**The good version:**

> Starting as a Product Designer at Spotify next week. Couldn't be more excited about the team and the product.
>
> Quick shoutout to my friend Marcus who referred me — I owe you dinner. And to my manager at my last role, Priya, who always pushed me to think bigger about my career. I wouldn't have applied for this role without that conversation we had last summer.

**What changed:** Thirty tagged names is a notification bomb, not a thank-you. Nobody knows what they're being thanked for. The good version thanks two people and explains exactly why. Marcus and Priya will feel far more appreciated than any of the 30 people in a mass tag. Quality over quantity applies to gratitude too, especially in a new role LinkedIn post.

## What NOT to Do in Your Job Announcement

Let's make this a quick checklist you can scan before hitting "Post."

**Don't write a novel.** Your LinkedIn new job announcement should be readable in under 30 seconds. If it requires scrolling on mobile, you've lost most of your audience. Aim for 100-200 words. Seriously.

**Don't use "humbled."** You worked hard for this. You prepared, you interviewed, you probably stressed about it. That's not humbling — that's earning something. Say you're excited, thrilled, grateful, or fired up. All are more honest than "humbled."

**Don't tag people for engagement farming.** If you're tagging someone, it should be because you're directly thanking them for something specific. If you're tagging them because you want their network to see your post, that's not gratitude — it's marketing.

**Don't manufacture a journey.** Not every job change is an epic narrative. Sometimes you found a better opportunity and took it. That's fine. That's actually great. You don't need to frame a three-month job search as a hero's arc to make your announcement interesting.

**Don't overthink the hashtags.** Two or three relevant ones are fine. #NewJob #Excited #Blessed #Grateful #NeverGiveUp #KeepGoing #DreamsComeTrue is... a lot.

**Don't post on your first day.** You'll be too distracted to respond to comments, and the anxiety of starting a new job plus managing LinkedIn engagement is a recipe for a bad day.

## FAQ

### Should I announce my new job on LinkedIn?

Yes, in most cases. Your professional network wants to know where you've landed, and it's a natural way to reconnect with people you've lost touch with. The only exception might be if you're in a sensitive industry or your employer has asked you to wait. Otherwise, a brief, genuine LinkedIn new job announcement is always appropriate.

### How soon after starting should I post?

The sweet spot is about one to two weeks after your start date. This gives you enough time to settle in, confirm the role is real (first-week jitters are a thing), and maybe even mention something specific about the team or work. Posting before you start can feel premature, and waiting months makes it old news. If you want to [write a great LinkedIn post](/blog/how-to-write-linkedin-post), timing matters.

### Should I tag my new company and manager?

Tag the company page — it helps with visibility and your new employer will likely appreciate it. For your manager, use your judgment. If you've already built a rapport during the interview process and they're active on LinkedIn, a tag with a brief note is fine. If you haven't started yet and barely know them, skip it. Nobody wants to be tagged in a post by someone they've met twice.

### How many hashtags should I use?

Three to five is the sweet spot. Stick to relevant ones like #NewJob, your industry, and maybe your company name. Anything more than five starts to look like you're gaming the algorithm, which — let's be honest — is exactly what those 15-hashtag posts are doing.
