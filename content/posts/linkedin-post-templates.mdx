**Filled-in example:**

### Thought Leadership Templates (5-8)

These **LinkedIn post templates** help you share opinions and insights without sounding like a TED Talk reject.

**5. Contrarian Take / Hot Take**

```
[Bold, slightly provocative statement]

I know this is unpopular, but [CONTRARIAN OPINION].

Here's why: [EVIDENCE/EXPERIENCE — 2-3 short paragraphs].

The counterargument is [ACKNOWLEDGE THE OTHER SIDE].
But in my experience, [REINFORCE YOUR POINT].

Am I wrong? Tell me why in the comments.

#[TOPIC] #[INDUSTRY] #Leadership
```

**Filled-in example:**

> Unpopular opinion: "Move fast and break things" is terrible advice for 95% of companies.
>
> I know this is unpopular, but speed without direction just means you break things faster. I've worked at three startups that wore velocity as a badge of honor. Two of them shipped features nobody wanted because they never slowed down long enough to talk to customers.
>
> Here's what works better: move deliberately and fix things. Spend the first week of any project talking to users. Spend the second week scoping ruthlessly. Then move fast on the thing that actually matters.
>
> The counterargument is that over-planning kills momentum. That's fair — analysis paralysis is real. But in my experience, one week of customer research saves three months of building the wrong thing.
>
> Am I wrong? Tell me why in the comments.
>
> #ProductManagement #Startups #Leadership

## How to Customize Templates Without Sounding Generic

Having a template is step one. Making it sound like you — not like a LinkedIn bot — is step two. Here are four rules to follow every time you use one of these **LinkedIn post templates**.

**Replace every placeholder with painfully specific details.** The difference between a generic post and a great post is specificity. "I learned a lot" is forgettable. "I learned that customers will forgive a buggy product but not a slow support response" is memorable. Every time you see a [PLACEHOLDER], replace it with a detail that only you could have written.

**Add your actual voice.** Do you use humor? Be funny. Are you more analytical? Lean into the data. Are you naturally understated? Keep it dry. If you are not sure what your voice sounds like, try writing a [LinkedIn post from scratch](/blog/how-to-write-linkedin-post) first, then compare it to the template. The template should match your energy, not the other way around.

**Use our [LinkedIn Translator](/#translator) to adjust the tone.** Wrote something that feels too corporate? Drop it into the translator and dial the intensity down. Wrote something that feels too casual for your audience? Crank it up. The tool lets you test different tonal levels before you hit Post — it is like a dress rehearsal for your writing.

**Test different intensity levels.** Not every post needs to be at max LinkedIn energy. A vulnerability post might work best at intensity 3 (honest and understated). A product launch might warrant intensity 7 (confident and polished). Experiment until you find the sweet spot for each template type. ## FAQ

**Can I use LinkedIn post templates word for word?**

Technically, yes — but you shouldn't. Templates work best as starting structures, not finished products. If you copy a template verbatim, it will read like a template. The whole point is to fill in your own specific details, swap out the placeholder language, and inject your personal perspective. Think of it like a Mad Libs game where the blanks are the most important part.

**How often should I post on LinkedIn?**

Two to three times per week is the sweet spot for most professionals. LinkedIn's algorithm rewards consistency more than frequency. Posting once a week every week is better than posting five times in one week and then going silent for a month. Pick three **LinkedIn post templates** from this list, rotate through them, and you will have a sustainable rhythm without burning out.

**Do LinkedIn post templates work for different industries?**

Absolutely. The templates in this collection are intentionally industry-agnostic. A "Lesson Learned" post works whether you're in fintech, healthcare, education, or construction. The structure — hook, story, lesson, CTA — is universal. What makes it industry-specific is the details you fill in. A nurse's "behind the scenes" post looks very different from a software engineer's, even if they're using the same template.

**Should I include hashtags in my LinkedIn posts?**

Yes, but keep it to three to five relevant hashtags. LinkedIn's algorithm uses hashtags to categorize your content and show it to people who follow those topics. Avoid overly broad hashtags like #Success or #Motivation — they're too competitive to help you. Instead, pick hashtags that match your niche: #DataEngineering, #ProductManagement, #HealthcareTech, and so on. Place them at the end of your post so they don't interrupt the reading flow.
